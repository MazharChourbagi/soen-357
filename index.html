UX/UI Design Case Study

Empowering Patients with
HealthSync

A comprehensive mobile app designed to help individuals with chronic conditions manage medications, track health, and seamlessly connect with healthcare professionals.

1. Clear Understanding of the Problem

Context & Objectives

The Challenge

Living with chronic health conditions requires rigorous daily management. Patients often struggle with medication adherence due to forgetfulness, get overwhelmed by tracking physical symptoms, and face friction when trying to schedule appointments. Current solutions are highly fragmented: a patient might use one app for alarms, a notebook for blood pressure, and calling the clinic for appointments. This fragmentation leads to cognitive overload and poor health outcomes.

The Solution

HealthSync is a unified "Super App" designed as a central hub for chronic care. It combines an intelligent medication reminder system, a simple symptom tracker, and a direct communication portal to healthcare providers. By centralizing these features into a highly accessible, WCAG-compliant interface, we reduce the cognitive load on the user and foster better communication with their care team.

2. Evidence of User Research

Methodology & Insights

Qualitative User Interviews (n=5)

Conducted deep-dive, 1-on-1 interviews with 5 individuals (ages 42-68) managing chronic conditions to uncover daily routines, emotional triggers, and root frustrations with current healthcare tech.

"I have 4 different pill bottles. Half the time I just stare at them trying to remember if I already took the morning ones. It's terrifying to accidentally double dose."
— Participant 3 (62, Diabetic)

Secondary Market Research

Analyzed top 5 competitor apps and medical UX journals. Discovered that complex UI (hidden menus, tiny touch targets) is the primary reason senior patients abandon health apps within 7 days. Also noted a lack of direct doctor-patient messaging in 80% of current apps.

Key Research Insights Synthesized

  • Accessibility is Non-Negotiable: Older users require larger typography (minimum 16pt body), high contrast ratios (WCAG AA), and unmistakable, labeled iconography.
  • Notification Fatigue: Standard iOS/Android notifications are easily swiped away and forgotten. Reminders need to be actionable directly within the app's main feed.
  • Anxiety Around Data: Users feel anxious about communicating symptoms accurately between visits; they want to easily share logs directly with doctors to validate their experiences.

3. User Personas & Requirements

Humanizing the Data

Sarah Jenkins

Retired Teacher • 68 years old • Type 2 Diabetes

"I just want to be sure I took the right pill so I don't bother my daughter about it."

Tech Savviness: Low
Goals: To maintain independence, adhere strictly to her insulin schedule, and easily show her doctor her logs without carrying a notebook.
Pain Points: Struggles with arthritis (small buttons are hard to tap) and mild vision loss. Extremely fearful of accidental double-dosing.
App Requirements: Progressive disclosure (show only what's needed *now*), massive touch targets, and visual timelines showing past doses.

Mark Davis

Software Engineer • 35 years old • Hypertension

"I'm too busy during the workday to be put on hold by a receptionist for 20 minutes."

Tech Savviness: High
Goals: Quickly manage his condition without disrupting his workday. Needs frictionless remote telehealth check-ins.
Pain Points: Hates calling clinics to book appointments. Often swipes away standard phone notifications during meetings and forgets to take pills later.
App Requirements: Integrated calendar system for automated booking, asynchronous chat with doctors, and persistent "Snooze" functionality for meds.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes & Structural Iteration

The transition from sketch to digital wireframe revealed a major flaw in my initial dashboard concept. I had to pivot the design architecture to account for our elderly persona's motor constraints and cognitive load.

Failed Iteration

Iteration 1: Multi-Widget Dashboard

UX Flaw: The grid layout created Hick's Law issues (too many choices leading to decision paralysis). Touch targets for the widgets were only 30px, well below the 44px WCAG recommendation for users with limited motor function.

Approved Direction

Iteration 2: Prioritized Feed Layout

The Solution: Implemented Progressive Disclosure. A single-column hierarchy places the most urgent action (e.g., "Take Morning Meds") in a massive, unmistakable card at the top, eliminating confusion.

6. UI Design System & Architecture

Visual Choices Rooted in UX Principles

Color Palette Psychology

The palette evokes calm and trust. We intentionally avoided standard "Alert Red" for notifications, opting instead for a warm orange to prompt action without inducing medical panic.

Typography for Accessibility

Inter was selected for its exceptional readability on screens, generous x-height, and clean geometric curves, vital for preventing eye strain in older demographics. Base size was set to 16px.

Aa
Inter Regular, Medium, Bold

Flat Navigation Architecture

Research showed that seniors struggle deeply with hidden menus (like the "hamburger" menu). To ensure a flat learning curve, the app relies on a persistent, clearly labeled bottom navigation bar containing only 4 primary destinations.

8. Usability Testing & Iteration

Validating the Prototype

Testing Methodology

I conducted moderated, task-based usability testing using the "Think-Aloud" protocol with 5 participants representing our target demographic (ages 45-70). A clickable prototype was provided using Figma Mirror on mobile devices.

Primary Tasks Evaluated:
  1. Find the schedule and mark the morning medication as "Taken".
  2. Navigate to the chat section to read a message from the doctor.
  3. Accept the doctor's invitation to book a telehealth appointment.
Quantitative Metrics Tracked:
  • Task Success Rate: Reached 100% after Iteration 2.
  • Time on Task: Decreased by 40% after removing hidden menus.
  • System Usability Scale (SUS): Achieved a score of 88 (Excellent).

Qualitative Feedback & Resulting Design Changes

  • Feedback: "The icons on the bottom are nice, but I'm not entirely sure what the calendar icon means."
    Action Taken: I added persistent text labels (Home, Meds, Visits, Chat) directly underneath all navigation icons. This immediately resolved navigation hesitation in subsequent tests.
  • Feedback: "When I click 'Log as Taken', I want to be absolutely sure it went through."
    Action Taken: I introduced a micro-interaction. Upon tapping the button, the row compresses slightly and a prominent green checkmark pops into the UI accompanied by a brief haptic vibration.

9. Final Reflection

Challenges & Personal Growth

Going through the complete UX design process for HealthSync was deeply illuminating. The most significant realization was that you are not your user. Designing for chronic healthcare requires putting aside modern, trendy UI patterns (like ultra-minimalist iconography or hidden swipe gestures) in favor of absolute clarity and accessibility.

What Was Challenging

Balancing a robust feature set (chat, calendar, reminders) without overwhelming the user interface was difficult. Early wireframes proved that putting too much on the screen caused decision paralysis for senior users. I had to practice strict information architecture to surface only what was immediately necessary.

What I Learned

The User Journey mapping directly influenced the app's notification strategy. Realizing the user's emotional state (anxiety around running out of pills) allowed me to design empathetic notifications that immediately offered a solution ("Book Refill") rather than just a warning.
