UX/UI Design Case Study

Empowering Patients with
HealthSync

A comprehensive mobile app designed to help individuals with chronic conditions manage medications, track health, and seamlessly connect with healthcare professionals.

1. Clear Understanding of the Problem

Context & Objectives

The Challenge

Living with chronic health conditions (like Diabetes, Hypertension, or Asthma) requires rigorous daily management. Patients often struggle with medication adherence due to forgetfulness, get overwhelmed by tracking physical symptoms, and face friction when trying to schedule appointments or communicate with their doctors. Current solutions are highly fragmented across different, unintuitive apps.

The Solution

HealthSync is a unified "Super App" designed as a central hub for chronic care. It combines an intelligent medication reminder system, a simple symptom tracker, and a direct communication portal to healthcare providers, all wrapped in a highly accessible and empathetic user interface.

2. Evidence of User Research

Methodology & Insights

User Interviews (n=5)

Conducted deep-dive, 1-on-1 interviews with 5 individuals (ages 42-68) managing chronic conditions like Diabetes and Hypertension to uncover daily routines and root frustrations.

"I have 4 different pill bottles. Half the time I just stare at them trying to remember if I already took the morning ones. It's terrifying to accidentally double dose."
— Participant 3 (62, Diabetic)

Secondary Research

Analyzed competitor apps and medical journals. Found that complex UI is the primary reason senior patients abandon health apps within 7 days.

Key Research Insights

  • Accessibility is non-negotiable: Older users require larger typography, high contrast, and unmistakable iconography.
  • Notification Fatigue: Users ignore standard app notifications. Reminders need to be actionable (e.g., "Take Meds Now" vs "Snooze").
  • Anxiety around Appointments: Users feel anxious about their health data between visits; they want to easily share symptom logs directly with doctors.

3. User Personas & Requirements

Synthesizing the Data

Sarah Jenkins

Retired Teacher • 68 years old • Type 2 Diabetes

Goals: To stay independent, remember to take her insulin on time, and easily show her blood sugar logs to her doctor.
Pain Points: Finds modern smartphones confusing. Small text is hard to read. Worries about pressing the wrong button and losing her data.
App Requirements: Large, legible fonts. A clear, loud daily alarm for meds. A simple "one-tap" button to log her current state.

Mark Davis

Software Engineer • 35 years old • Hypertension

Goals: Quickly manage his medication without disrupting his busy workday. Easily book remote telehealth check-ins.
Pain Points: Hates calling clinics to book appointments. Often swipes away notifications and forgets to take pills later.
App Requirements: Persistent reminders. An integrated calendar system for automated appointment booking and in-app doctor chat.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes & Failed Iterations

Before moving to high-fidelity, several low-fidelity layouts were explored. The initial concept for the dashboard failed to account for our elderly persona's motor constraints and cognitive load.

Failed Iteration

Iteration 1: Multi-Widget Dashboard

Why it failed: The grid layout created cognitive overload. Users didn't know what to look at first, and the touch targets for the widgets were too small for users with limited motor function.

Approved Direction

Iteration 2: Prioritized Feed Layout

The Solution: Adopted a single-column hierarchy. By placing the most urgent action (e.g., "Take Morning Meds") in a massive, unmistakable card at the top, we eliminated confusion and improved task success rates.

6. UI Design System & Architecture

Style, Color, Animation, Fonts & Navigation

Color Palette

The palette is designed to evoke calm, trust, and cleanliness, avoiding harsh alarms while ensuring high contrast for visually impaired users.

Typography

Inter was selected for its exceptional readability on screens, generous x-height, and clean geometric curves, vital for older demographics.

App Navigation Architecture (Bottom Tab Navigation)

To ensure a flat learning curve, the app relies on a persistent bottom navigation bar containing 4 primary destinations. Complex hamburger menus were avoided as research showed they confuse senior users.

8. Usability Testing

Testing the Prototype

Testing Plan & Execution

Conducted moderated, task-based usability testing with 5 participants representing our target demographic (ages 45-70). A clickable prototype was provided using Figma Mirror on mobile devices.

Primary Tasks Evaluated:
  1. Add a new prescription to the daily schedule.
  2. Log a missed dose and record the reason.
  3. Navigate to the chat and request an appointment.
Metrics Tracked:
  • Task Success Rate
  • Time on Task
  • System Usability Scale (SUS) Score

9. Final Reflection

Challenges & Takeaways

Going through the complete UX design process for HealthSync was deeply illuminating. The most significant realization was that you are not your user. Designing for chronic healthcare requires putting aside modern, trendy UI patterns (like ultra-minimalist iconography or hidden gestures) in favor of absolute clarity and accessibility.

Key Challenges

Balancing a robust feature set (chat, calendar, reminders) without overwhelming the user interface was difficult. I had to practice strict information architecture to surface only what was immediately necessary (e.g., placing the upcoming medication card front and center on the dashboard).

Value of the Process

The User Journey mapping directly influenced the app's notification strategy. Realizing the user's emotional state (anxiety around running out of pills) allowed me to design empathetic notifications that immediately offered a solution ("Book Refill") rather than just a warning.
